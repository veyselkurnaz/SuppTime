Most people build their supplement routine one bottle at a time — a multivitamin here, a magnesium for sleep there — without ever mapping out how those pieces interact once they're all being swallowed in the same 10-minute window every morning. The result is usually a stack that quietly cancels itself out: calcium blocking the iron you just took for anemia, zinc competing with the copper your body also needs, or a fish oil capsule passing through completely unabsorbed because it was taken on an empty stomach.

None of these failures show up as symptoms you'd immediately connect to timing. A blood test might flag low ferritin months after you started an iron supplement, and the actual cause — a calcium-fortified breakfast eaten in the same sitting — is easy to miss because both products are, individually, doing exactly what they're supposed to do. The problem is never the supplement; it's the schedule.

This guide is the map. Below are the three practical clusters that matter for most people's stacks, each linking to the full clinical breakdown — absorption mechanisms, dosing windows, and the specific drug and food interactions — for that nutrient. ## Cluster 1: Interactions & Mineral Safety

The minerals in this cluster physically compete with each other for the same intestinal transporters. Taking any two of them in the same sitting can silently cut absorption of one or both by 40-60%. This is the highest-stakes cluster because the failure mode is invisible — you keep taking a supplement that isn't reaching your bloodstream, and the only signal is a lab result that doesn't improve as expected.

## Cluster 2: Absorption Science (Fat- and Water-Soluble Vitamins)

These nutrients depend on specific digestive conditions — dietary fat, stomach acid, or an empty stomach — to be absorbed at all. Unlike Cluster 1, the issue here usually isn't one nutrient blocking another; it's a mismatch between the supplement's chemistry and what's currently in your stomach, which means the fix is almost always about *when* and *with what*, not *which brand*.

## Cluster 3: Structural & Form Selection

Not every chemical form of a supplement behaves the same way in the body — the right form matters as much as the timing. Two people can buy "the same" supplement, in the sense that the label lists the same active ingredient, and get meaningfully different results because the underlying compound isn't identical.

## The Three Rules Worth Memorizing

If you only take a few things away from this guide:

1. **Never stack two minerals in the same sitting.** Calcium, iron, zinc, and magnesium all compete for the same transport pathways. Space them at least 2 hours apart.
2. **Match the nutrient to its digestive requirement.** Fat-soluble vitamins (D, K2, omega-3) need dietary fat. Water-soluble ones (B12, vitamin C) generally don't, and some — like iron and zinc — actively prefer an empty stomach despite the digestive trade-offs involved.
3. **Chemical form is not a footnote.** "Magnesium" or "collagen" on a label describes a category, not a single compound — the specific salt or peptide type determines whether it does what you actually bought it for.
