But once you actually start using one daily, the differences that matter aren't cosmetic — they're about whether the app understands the *chemistry* of what you're taking, not just the schedule.

## What Most Tracking Apps Get Wrong

A reminder that fires at 8 AM and 8 PM is trivial to build — dozens of generic habit-tracker apps already do this for medication, water intake, or exercise. The actual hard problem in supplement tracking is different: **knowing which combinations quietly cancel each other out, and which ones should never be taken in the same sitting.**

Calcium blocking iron absorption. Levothyroxine interacting with nearly every mineral. Coffee cutting non-heme iron uptake by up to 70%. These aren't edge cases — they're common stacks that most people build by accident, one supplement bottle at a time, with no single tool checking the combination.

## Where a Dedicated Interaction Engine Changes the Outcome

The difference between "I take my vitamins" and "I take my vitamins effectively" usually comes down to three things:

1. **Sequencing** — some nutrients compete for the same absorption pathway and need to be spaced apart, not stacked together.
2. **Food context** — fat-soluble vitamins require dietary fat to absorb at all; water-soluble ones often don't care.
3. **Medication awareness** — many common prescriptions (thyroid hormone, PPIs, metformin, bisphosphonates) have well-documented supplement interactions that a generic reminder app has no way of flagging.

A tracker that only handles reminders will never catch any of this. A tracker built around an interaction engine — checked against real, citable research — can.

## Summary

Reminders solve forgetting. They don't solve the more expensive mistake: taking the wrong combination, at the wrong time, for months, without ever knowing it was working against you. That's the actual feature to evaluate when comparing supplement apps — not the reminder bell, but what happens the moment you add a second and third supplement to your daily stack.
